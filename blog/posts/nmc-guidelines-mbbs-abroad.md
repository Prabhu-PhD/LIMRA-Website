If you plan to study medicine abroad and practise in India afterwards, the rules set by the National Medical Commission (NMC) decide whether your degree will count. Getting these right *before* you choose a country or university saves enormous trouble later. Here's a plain-English overview of the areas every aspirant should check.

## 1. Eligibility starts with NEET

To pursue a primary medical qualification abroad and later register in India, Indian students are generally required to have qualified the NEET examination. Treat a valid NEET result as the baseline gate — without it, recognition of a foreign medical degree becomes a serious problem.

## 2. Course structure and duration

The NMC expects a foreign medical course to be broadly comparable to the Indian MBBS — covering the same core subjects, with adequate clinical training, over a duration aligned to its norms. Some norms also expect the course, clinical training and internship to be completed in the **same country** where you studied. Because the specifics have changed in recent years, confirm the current expectations for your chosen country before enrolling.

## 3. The licensing exam

A foreign degree does not by itself let you practise in India. You must clear the screening examination — currently the Foreign Medical Graduate Examination (FMGE), with a planned transition toward the National Exit Test (NExT). Passing it is what converts your overseas degree into eligibility for registration with a State Medical Council or the NMC.

## 4. Choose a recognised university

Not every overseas university produces a degree the NMC will accept. Look for institutions whose programs are designed to meet NMC norms and that appear in recognised international directories. This single decision — picking a genuinely recognised university — protects everything that follows.

### Before you apply, confirm that:

- You have a valid NEET qualification
- The university and its course meet current NMC norms
- The course, clinical training and internship arrangements fit the rules
- You understand the screening exam (FMGE / NExT) you'll need to clear
- You have a realistic plan to prepare for that exam from year one
